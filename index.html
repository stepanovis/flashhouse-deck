The agency already runs
full-cycle. By hand.

The full cycle already works.

In mature bureaucratic markets, the agency is the trusted intermediary end to end — a tenant can rent, or a buyer can close, without ever meeting the other side. That's not chaos. That's a real, valuable service.

It's just done by hand.

Calls, photos, listings, showings, filings, disclosures — every one of those steps is manual, redone at every handoff, owned by no single system.

Owners fly blind.

No real-time view of the pipeline, no analytics, no way to manage the business — only the people inside it.

Result: a service worth paying for, delivered at manual cost — high price per deal, long cycles, delays at every handoff, errors that compound.

Not automation bolted on.
Process built for AI first.

Every operation the agency runs — search, listing, showing, follow-up — is designed around an AI agent and a clean UI from day one, not a human workflow with software glued on top.

Driven to zero

Cost per deal, time to close, handoff delays, error rate.

Driven to max

Conversion, client access, and how easy it is to see and manage the whole business.

Peru's count isn't sourced directly — no public INDECOPI/ministry total exists — it's Brazil's agencies-per-capita ratio applied to Peru's population, a proxy pending real field data. ARPU is a placeholder from FlashHouse's base assumptions, not yet measured on a signed contract anywhere.

  • The screen — licensed agents with real liability for the deal (not an unaccountable pass-through); fragmented SMB agencies, not one dominant chain; enough transaction volume to matter; and no captive monopoly — a market where a single capitalized portal already owns agent traffic is a dead end, however big the paperwork.
  • Wave one — Brazil. Discovery is already portal-owned (QuintoAndar, VivaReal/ZAP) but the agency layer is independent and thriving — exactly the pattern we displace, one agency at a time. Political risk: October 2026 general elections and fiscal instability are real (Eurasia Group, Allianz Trade, Deloitte) — but a CRM sold on agency operating cost is far less exposed to that cycle than a developer or a fund would be; a slower market makes efficiency tools more necessary, not less.
  • Wave two — Peru first — of the shortlist (Peru, Mexico, Argentina, Morocco, Vietnam), Peru leads: similar mentality and sales approach to Brazil, Spanish/Portuguese-adjacent, and — unlike Mexico (QuintoAndar already present) — no dominant funded incumbent yet. Mexico and Argentina follow (notary-mediated, but state-by-state licensing / macro risk respectively); Morocco and Vietnam are further out on cultural and operational distance.
  • Deprioritized — Colombia, Thailand: no real agent licensing, same unaccountable pattern we screen out. Chile: likely already too digitized. Indonesia, Nigeria, Kenya, Philippines: bureaucracy without a mature accountable intermediary.
  • Why this order — licensed, accountable agents already think in ROI and buy efficiency on the numbers; no education needed to sell to them. Markets with no agent accountability, or captive to one dominant state-linked portal (our reference case: Russia's DomClick/Sber), aren't closed forever — just sequenced after outside proof lands elsewhere, when the sale gets easier there too.

Real reference cases,

3 agencies committed in Brazil today (verbal, not yet contracted — early and real), more through Vlad's network. Each one becomes a case study with real before/after metrics — conversion, cost per deal, time to close.

02 · Realtor associations

Warm intros,
not cold outbound

Target SECOVI — a trade association (23 regional chapters), not the licensing regulator CRECI, which we deliberately don't pursue as a commercial partner (it's a regulator, and some regional CRECI/sector bodies have faced antitrust scrutiny). Structure: revenue-share on referred agencies (~10-15% of first-year value) plus event sponsorship, not a big upfront fee. One SECOVI chapter presentation reaches dozens of agencies in a single session — far cheaper per lead than a cold field-sales meeting, and the referral converts warmer.

03 · Shared network effect

Whoever shows it first
has the edge

In Brazil, the agent who first shows a listing to a buyer holds the claim on that sale — so speed to close is worth real money, and agencies with a shared, faster-moving base outperform ones without it. Buyers still shop around, which is exactly why closing faster matters.

04 · Field sales

This is a relationship sale.
We show up in person

Agency owners buy from people they've met, not a landing page. One city first, to prove the motion (~3 reps, ~4 months). Once it holds, roll out in parallel to 9 more key cities — 10 cities × 3 reps, ~30 people — rather than scale one city at a time. Each rep: ~3 demos/day, ~60/month; at 30% meeting→trial (SMB field-sales benchmark) and 20% trial→paying (top of the SMB SaaS range, justified by in-person demos), that's ~3.6 new agencies/month once ramped — before adding SECOVI-warmed leads on top.

  • Sell metrics, not features — design-partner numbers become the pitch for the next agency: here's what your conversion and cost per deal look like on FlashHouse.
  • No cold migration — agencies aren't starting from nothing: WhatsApp threads, Excel sheets, whatever they already run on. Onboarding means the AI ingests that mess directly — no manual re-entry — and the agent keeps working in WhatsApp during a parallel-run window instead of learning a new app on day one.
  • Land on the CRM, expand into the rest — agencies onboard on the core; concierge showcase and field app upsell once the CRM is the system of record.
  • Sequenced by market, not simultaneous — Tranche 1 proves the motion in Brazil; Tranche 2 repeats it in the Wave two shortlist with a working playbook instead of a guess.

Traction target: a realistic dozen design partners in Brazil within the design-partner phase, per the team's own estimate — the reference base the association and network motions build on. Field-sales conversion rates (25% / 40%) are our working assumption, not measured yet — the first reps validate or correct them.

No one's built this
for this niche yet. That won't last.

  • The gap is specific, not generic — an AI-native storefront and CRM built for licensed, accountable SMB agencies in bureaucratic markets doesn't exist yet. It's an obvious build once someone looks — which is exactly the risk.
  • Buyer behavior shifted first — conversational shopping is mainstream elsewhere; local real estate portals still run on forms and filters.
  • Inference got cheap enough — running a concierge AI per listing is now a unit-economics-positive move, not a research project.
  • The window is narrow — once a funded player or a portal incumbent builds this, the wedge closes. We move on founder-market fit and a live engine now, before it's obvious to everyone else.

The window closes once a local incumbent bolts a chatbot onto its existing portal — a feature, not a rebuild of who owns the client.

On QuintoAndar specifically: in 2023–2024 QuintoAndar itself pivoted away from displacing agencies — its "marketplace of marketplaces" model now partners with them, and reportedly doubled partner-agency sales. Worth naming directly: Loft. Loft ($2.2B valuation, $798M raised) pivoted from iBuying into a B2B SaaS + fintech platform for ~7,000 partner agencies, with an AI layer already in production for lead qualification and pricing, plus a broker-facing CRM. A headline valuation that size is as much proof the category is real as it is a competitive threat — and it's fintech-monetized (rent guarantees, mortgage origination fund most of its revenue), tooling for agencies that want to plug into its financial products. FlashHouse is concierge-first and conversational — the AI-native buyer storefront and the field-capture app aren't in Loft's feature set — aimed at the long tail of independent 3-10 agent shops Loft's model doesn't chase. The category being contested this hard is the market entering the door, not a reason to wait at it.
